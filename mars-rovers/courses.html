Mission History

NASA’s Mars rovers are launched from Earth on powerful rockets and travel millions of kilometers through space before arriving at Mars. Each rover’s mission has a unique landing site and scientific focus.

Spirit & Opportunity

Launch: 2003 (separate launches for each rover)

Landing: January 2004, different locations on Mars

Spirit landed in Gusev Crater and Opportunity landed on the opposite side of Mars in Meridiani Planum. Their missions were designed to last about 90 Martian days (sols), but both rovers far outlived their planned lifetimes.

They found strong evidence that Mars once had liquid water, such as rocks that formed in water and signs of past wet environments.

Curiosity

Launch: November 2011

Landing: August 2012 in Gale Crater

Curiosity used a dramatic “sky crane” landing system to touch down safely in Gale Crater. This site was chosen because it has layers of rock that record Mars’s environmental history.

Over time, Curiosity has climbed the lower slopes of Mount Sharp, studying rock layers that show how Mars changed from a wetter world to the cold, dry planet we see today.

Perseverance

Launch: July 2020

Landing: February 2021 in Jezero Crater

Perseverance used a sky crane system, landing in Jezero Crater, which is believed to be an ancient lakebed with an old river delta. This makes it a prime location to search for signs of past microbial life.

In addition to its scientific tools, Perseverance carries instruments to test new technologies, such as making oxygen from the Martian atmosphere and studying how future astronauts might use local resources.
